What happens to the trillions in boomer-owned business equity that's supposed to change hands in the next decade? Mostly, it doesn't. Most of those for-sale listings never make it to closing and that's an unfortunate state of affairs.

Somewhere around three-quarters of small businesses that go up for sale never transact.  A lot of that is self-inflicted: businesses get listed at 5 to 8 times revenue when real Main Street multiples run closer to 1x, sometimes less. That's not an aggressive buyer it's a listing that was DOA before it went live.

A house that sits unsold for a year is still worth roughly what it was worth the year before. A business doesn't get that floor, because its value lives entirely in the cash flow, and cash flow depends on an owner who's still showing up. The longer a listing drags, the more risk that value decays toward zero.

Brokers are part of the problem too, wildly inconsistent as a category, often incentivized to just get a listing signed rather than push back on an unrealistic price. The fix starts well before anything goes to market: clean books, honest add-backs, and increasingly a sell-side quality of earnings report before a buyer's diligence team finds the problems for you.
