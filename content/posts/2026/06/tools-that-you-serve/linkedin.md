What if the tools we build to solve our problems eventually restructure the environment so completely that we can't solve those problems any other way?

There's a pattern that shows up across centuries and domains: we build a tool to serve us, it scales, and somewhere past a certain things flip, and we're serving it.

This isn't a technology problem. It shows up in 19th-century Prussian forestry, postwar American urban planning, and the Soviet economy. The same thing happened to social media. And it's now setting up to happen with AI.

A philosopher named Ivan Illich named the mechanism in 1973. He called it the flip from convivial tools to industrial tools. A convivial tool you control entirely. An industrial tool, past a certain threshold of scale, starts shaping you to serve it. It requires experts, creates dependency, and eventually becomes counterproductive, undermining the very purpose it was built for. Schools produce credential-dependence instead of education. Medicine medicalizes ordinary life until people can't navigate it without supervision. Cars restructured American cities so thoroughly there was nowhere left to walk.

He called the end state radical monopoly: not one company controlling a market, but a tool restructuring the environment so alternatives stop being viable entirely. The social graph you've built on any major platform today isn't portable. The communities there exist nowhere else. That's radical monopoly, delivered by software.

AI right now looks like the early internet: open APIs, local models, the ability to switch providers. But Microsoft is embedding Copilot into Office. Google is weaving Gemini into Search and Android. Apple is building intelligence into the OS. None of those are convivial designs. They're platforms using AI to deepen the integration that already made them hard to leave. Once enough work and thinking flows through these systems, the capacity to do those things without them starts to atrophy, the same way navigation did after GPS and memory did after search engines. Building composably, with clean interfaces that keep components separable, is harder than building monoliths and goes against the economic incentives. It has to be a deliberate choice.

The GLP-1 wave is worth watching for the same reason. These drugs work for severe obesity, but what they actually suppress is the reward-motivation signal broadly, not just hunger. We're medicating the experience of wanting things, at population scale, and we don't yet know what's load-bearing.
