On March 31, Google Quantum AI published a paper suggesting a cryptographically relevant quantum computer might be achievable with fewer than 500k physical qubits (well below previous estimates). Their suggested migration target: 2029(!).

That's not a distant theoretical risk. That's three years.

The encryption protecting HTTPS, VPNs, SSH, banking infrastructure, and most of the internet's trust model relies on math that a sufficiently powerful quantum computer can break. And here's the part that's already happening: nation-state adversaries are almost certainly collecting encrypted traffic today with the intent to decrypt it retroactively once the capability exists. If your data needs to stay confidential for more than a few years, the clock is already running.

NIST finalized the first post-quantum cryptography standards in August 2024. Networking vendors are starting to ship implementations. But "standards exist" and "deployed everywhere" are normally separated by a decade or more. New hardware, software upgrades, interoperability testing, and operational disruption across every layer of the stack.

Cryptocurrency is a harder problem. Around 28–35% of Bitcoin's total supply sits in addresses where the public key is already visible on-chain, directly attackable by a CRQC. Migrating Bitcoin's signature scheme requires decentralized consensus across miners, developers, exchanges, and millions of users.  Core developers estimate 5 to 10 years from activation to full ecosystem migration. The governance debate is happening right now, but it's still at the conceptual stage.

The realistic outcome isn't that we migrate everything in time. It's that we triage: protect the highest-value, highest-sensitivity systems first and accept that long-tail infrastructure will remain exposed longer than is comfortable.
